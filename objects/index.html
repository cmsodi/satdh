    Spacecraft:

    A spacecraft is a vehicle or machine intended for space travel. It comprises a vast array of objects, including satellites, probes, rovers, landers, and human-rated vehicles such as the International Space Station (ISS) and the Space Shuttle. It may also contain smaller components, such as modules and subsystems, that are utilized in larger spacecraft. The term “spacecraft” encompasses all space-bound vehicles and machines.

    Launch Base (LaunchB):

    A launch base is a facility that is used to launch a launch vehicle into space. The launch bases may be terrestrial, maritime, or airborne.

    • Ground-based launch bases are facilities located on the surface of the Earth that are used to launch rockets and spacecraft into outer space. Typically, they contain launch pads, fuel storage facilities, and other facilities essential for launching spacecraft.

    • Naval launch bases are facilities that are located on ships or submarines and are used to launch rockets or missiles, typically submarine-launched ballistic missiles (SLBMs) and other types of missiles. They provide a mobile platform for launching rockets or missiles and can be used to launch from various locations in the ocean.

    • Airborne launch bases are facilities that are mounted on aircraft or airships and are used to launch rockets or missiles. Typically, they are utilized for aerial reconnaissance and surveillance missions as well as the delivery of space payloads.

    Launch vehicle (LaunchV):

    A launch vehicle is any vehicle or machine that is used to deliver a payload into space. This can include rockets as well as Spaceplanes, Balloons, and Launchers attached to aircraft, among others.

    • A rocket is a vehicle or apparatus that uses rocket engines to propel itself through the atmosphere or into space. Numerous sizes and shapes of rockets exist, ranging from miniature model rockets used for hobby or educational purposes to massive rockets used for launching satellites, crewed spacecraft, and other payloads into orbit or beyond.

    • Spaceplanes are aircraft-like craft that are designed to take off from a runway and fly into space. After completing their mission, they can land on a runway and operate similarly to conventional aircraft.

    • Balloons are large, high-altitude balloons that can transport cargo into the upper atmosphere or near-space environment. They carry scientific instruments or cameras to collect data about the atmosphere or space environment.

    • Launchers attached to aircraft are payloads that are attached to aircraft and released from great heights. The payload is then ignited in order to enter space. This method is less expensive than conventional rockets and can carry smaller payloads.

    Space Station (SpaceSt):

    A space station is a satellite or facility constructed by humans that orbits a celestial body or is located on its surface and serves as a long-term habitat for crew members. It typically consists of living quarters, work areas, and diverse systems for life support, communication, and scientific research. Space stations are designed to be inhabited for extended durations and serve a variety of purposes, including scientific research, technology development, and international cooperation.

    They may be visited by crew members on a rotating basis and resupplied on a regular basis with food, water, and other essential supplies. Space stations offer a one-of-a-kind environment for conducting research in microgravity or harsh extraterrestrial environments, as well as for observing and studying celestial bodies and their surroundings.

    Earth station (EarthSt):

    Earth stations are ground-based facilities used for communication and control of satellites, spacecraft, and other objects in space. They are indispensable to the operation of satellite-based communication systems, navigation systems, and Earth observation systems. Earth stations can be either fixed or mobile and can be operated by government agencies, commercial companies, or other organizations.
